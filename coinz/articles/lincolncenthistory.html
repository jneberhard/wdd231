The History of the Lincoln Cent—and Its Uncertain Future

The Lincoln cent, often called the "penny," has been a fixture of American currency since 1909. From its origins as a tribute to one of the nation’s greatest presidents to the modern-day debate over its relevance, the story of the Lincoln cent is a fascinating journey through American history, economics, and culture.

A Presidential Tribute: The Birth of the Lincoln Cent (1909)

In 1909, the U.S. Mint introduced the Lincoln cent to commemorate the 100th anniversary of Abraham Lincoln’s birth. It became the first regular U.S. coin to feature a real person, replacing the long-running Indian Head cent.

Designed by Victor David Brenner, the coin’s obverse (front) featured a right-facing profile of Lincoln. The reverse displayed two wheat ears and the words "One Cent", "United States of America", and "E Pluribus Unum". Brenner’s initials (V.D.B.) were initially prominent but quickly removed due to public criticism, later reappearing more subtly in 1918.

A New Look: The Lincoln Memorial Reverse (1959)

To celebrate Lincoln’s 150th birthday, the coin received a new reverse design in 1959. Created by Frank Gasparro, it featured the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C., making it the first coin to depict the same individual on both sides (Lincoln is visible inside the memorial).

This design remained unchanged for 50 years.

1982: A Shift in Composition

As copper prices rose in the early 1980s, the Mint changed the penny’s composition. Prior to 1982, pennies were made of 95% copper and 5% zinc. After mid-1982, they switched to 97.5% zinc with a thin copper plating. Both versions were minted that year, leading to several collectible varieties.

2009: Four Designs for Lincoln’s Bicentennial

To honor Lincoln’s 200th birthday and the centennial of the Lincoln cent, the Mint issued four commemorative reverse designs in 2009. Each highlighted a different phase of Lincoln’s life:

  • Birth and early childhood in Kentucky
  • Formative years in Indiana
  • Professional life in Illinois
  • Presidency in Washington, D.C.

These special editions were released throughout the year.

2010–Present: The Union Shield

In 2010, the Lincoln cent received a new permanent reverse design: the Union Shield, representing Lincoln’s legacy in preserving the nation during the Civil War. Designed by Lyndall Bass, the shield features 13 vertical stripes bound by a single horizontal bar, symbolizing unity.

This is the design currently in circulation today.

Should We Retire the Penny?

Despite its deep roots in American history, the Lincoln cent has become the center of an ongoing debate over whether it should be discontinued.

Why Some Say It’s Time to Say Goodbye:

  • It costs more than 1¢ to make. Producing each penny costs the U.S. Mint between 1.5–2.5 cents.
  • Inflation has made it nearly worthless. The cent buys far less than it did even a few decades ago.
  • It wastes time. Pennies slow down cash transactions and require handling, storage, and shipping.
  • Environmental concerns. Mining and refining metals for coins add to environmental strain.

Why Others Want to Keep It:

  • Rounding worries. Some fear removing the penny could lead to price rounding that hurts consumers.
  • Cultural and historical significance. Lincoln’s image on the coin is beloved by many.
  • Charitable use. Pennies are commonly used in fundraising and donation drives.

What Other Countries Are Doing

Countries like Canada, Australia, and New Zealand have already eliminated their lowest-denomination coins. In most cases, cash transactions are rounded to the nearest 5 cents, while electronic payments remain exact.

Conclusion

The Lincoln cent has served as a symbol of American ideals for over a century. But as production costs rise and its purchasing power diminishes, the future of the penny is uncertain. Whether it remains in circulation or fades into history, the Lincoln cent will always hold a unique place in the story of U.S. coinage.
