Lincoln’s Legacy in U.S. Currency

Few figures in American history are as universally admired and widely recognized as Abraham Lincoln. Known as the "Great Emancipator," Lincoln’s leadership during the Civil War and his unwavering commitment to unity and equality have left an enduring impact on the United States—and that legacy is literally in our hands every day.

From coins to paper currency, Lincoln’s image is a prominent and powerful reminder of the values he stood for. Here’s a look at how Abraham Lincoln is honored through U.S. money—and why his presence remains as relevant today as ever.

The Lincoln Cent (1909–Present)

Lincoln’s introduction to U.S. coinage began in 1909, marking the centennial of his birth. The Lincoln cent was the first American coin to feature a real person—a significant departure from previous designs that used allegorical figures like Liberty.

  • Designer: Victor David Brenner
  • Obverse: Lincoln’s right-facing profile
  • Reverse (1909–1958): Two wheat ears (the “wheat penny”)
  • Reverse (1959–2008): Lincoln Memorial
  • Reverse (2010–present): Union Shield

The Lincoln cent is the longest-running design in U.S. Mint history, and it continues to circulate more than a century later.

The Five-Dollar Bill

Lincoln also appears on the $5 bill, one of the most commonly used pieces of paper currency. His portrait has been featured on the five since the 1910s, with occasional updates to the layout, font, and security features.

  • Portrait: Centered, based on a Mathew Brady photograph
  • Reverse: The Lincoln Memorial
  • Current Series: Includes enhanced anti-counterfeiting features and color accents

Because of its wide circulation, the $5 bill is one of the most visible and enduring tributes to Lincoln’s legacy.

Lincoln in Commemorative Coins

Lincoln Bicentennial Cents (2009)

To celebrate both Lincoln’s 200th birthday and the 100th anniversary of the Lincoln cent, the Mint released four special reverses in 2009:

  • Birth and Early Childhood in Kentucky
  • Formative Years in Indiana
  • Professional Life in Illinois
  • Presidency in Washington, D.C.

Each design highlighted a key phase of Lincoln’s life and contributions.

Lincoln Presidential $1 Coin (2010)

Part of the Presidential $1 Coin Program, Lincoln’s golden dollar was released in 2010 and features his portrait on the obverse and the Statue of Liberty on the reverse.

Why Lincoln?

Abraham Lincoln’s inclusion on U.S. currency is more than symbolic—it’s a reflection of national values.

  • Unity: Lincoln preserved the Union during its most fragile period.
  • Equality: He issued the Emancipation Proclamation and laid the foundation for civil rights.
  • Leadership: His moral clarity and perseverance during crisis remain a gold standard for presidential leadership.

These themes resonate strongly with America’s identity, making Lincoln a timeless figure to feature on our money.

Public Sentiment and Trust

Interestingly, studies have shown that Americans tend to trust and respect currency featuring familiar, historical figures. Lincoln’s image is widely recognized and associated with honesty, justice, and resilience. His presence on money helps reinforce trust in the financial system itself.

Lincoln and the Future of Currency

Even as the U.S. explores the future of digital currency and debates the relevance of physical coins (such as whether to retire the penny), Lincoln’s legacy remains secure. His image is likely to endure—whether in your pocket or on your smartphone—as a lasting tribute to a man who shaped the soul of the nation.
