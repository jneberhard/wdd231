Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What does the PSWD mean in your name?
A: They are the letters for the current US mints: Philadelphia, San Francisco, West Point, Denver

-------

Q: What Are Coins Worth That You Get Back in Change?
A: As a general rule, coins you receive as change are worth their face value.
Exceptions: Rare or Collectible Coins
Some coins can be worth more due to their rarity, age, or unique features. Keep an eye out for:

  • Wheat pennies (minted before 1959)
  • Silver coins (dimes, quarters, and half dollars minted before 1965 contain 90% silver)
  • Error coins (like double strikes or misprints)
  • Low-mintage years (fewer coins produced, more valuable)
Also, look for the following:
  • Unusual designs or older dates
  • Mint marks (like “S” for San Francisco, or missing ones)
  • Coins in excellent condition (little to no wear)

-------

Q: Are coins a good investment?
A: Coins can be a good investment, but it depends on several factors:

  • Collector Value: Rare, historic, or limited-edition coins often increase in value over time.
  • Precious Metal Content: Coins made of silver, gold, or platinum can be valuable based on metal prices.
  • Market Knowledge: Successful coin investing requires knowledge about grading, authenticity, and market trends.
  • Patience: Coin investments usually grow slowly and aren’t as liquid as stocks or bonds.
  • Risk: Like any investment, there is risk — not all coins appreciate, and some might lose value.
If you're interested in coin collecting or investing, it’s wise to research thoroughly or consult with a professional coin dealer or numismatist.

Sense of the Cents

For more than a century, America’s lowest-denomination coins—the penny and the nickel—have sparked debates that go beyond pocket change. While they may seem insignificant in value, these small coins carry a larger story about national identity, economic efficiency, and the evolution of money itself.

So what’s the real“sense” of the cents? Do these coins still make financial sense—or are they simply relics of a bygone era?

A Penny’s Worth: Then and Now

When the Lincoln cent was first minted in1909,one cent could buy a loaf of bread or a scoop of candy. The nickel, introduced in1866had even broader utility. But today, thanks to inflation, these coins buy very little—if anything at all.

Yet they continue to be mintedby the billionseach year.

In 2023 alone, the U.S. Mint produced over4 billion pennies, despite growing concerns about their cost, utility, and environmental impact.

The Cost of Making Cents

Ironically, both the penny and the nickelcost more to producethan they are worth:

  • Penny: ~2.7¢ to make 1¢
  • Nickel: ~10.4¢ to make 5¢

Together, these coinscost taxpayers millions of dollars annually,simply because the cost of raw materials—like copper, zinc, and nickel—has outpaced their face value.

Do We Still Use Them?

Many Americans rarely use small coins in daily life. Cash usage is declining, and when people do pay with coins, they often leave pennies behind in tip jars or “take a penny, leave a penny” trays.

Retailers round prices, consumers round totals, and coin jars accumulate for years before being cashed in. While coins once played a vital role in everyday commerce, today their presence is often morehabitual than helpful.

Why Are They Still Around?

Despite their declining relevance, small coins persist—for a few key reasons:

  • Public sentiment:People are attached to coins that carry the image ofAbraham LincolnorThomas Jefferson.
  • Fear of rounding:Some worry that removing cents would lead toprice roundingthat subtly favors businesses.
  • Charity and tradition:Coins are still used indonation drives,educational projects, and coin collecting hobbies.
  • Industry lobbying:Metal suppliers and vending machine manufacturers have historically lobbied to keep coins in circulation.

Making Sense of Modern Coinage

The debate over cents is really a broader conversation about how our societyuses and values money. As digital payments, contactless cards, and mobile apps become the norm, physical cash—and coins in particular—play ashrinking rolein transactions.

Many other countries have alreadyphased out small-denomination coinsand moved torounding systemsfor cash purchases. These changes were met withminimal disruptionand considerable savings.

Looking Ahead: A Smarter System?

Here’s what a more efficient U.S. coin system could look like:

  • Eliminate the penny and possibly the nickel)
  • Round cash totalsto the nearest 5 cents
  • Keep digital and card payments exact
  • Use redesigned coinsfor special occasions (like the 2026 Semiquincentennial)
  • Focus productionon useful denominations like the dime, quarter, and dollar coin

Conclusion: Finding the Sense in Cents

Small coins may seem trivial, but they carry weight—historically, symbolically, and economically. Whether we keep them, change them, or phase them out, the decision should be guided by what trulymakes sensefor our future.

As we approach milestones like the250th anniversary of U.S. independence,perhaps it’s time toreevaluate not just how we use money—but how we value it.
