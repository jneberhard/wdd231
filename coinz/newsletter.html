Why the Penny May Soon Disappear

For over 100 years, the penny—officially known as the Lincoln cent—has been a fixture in American currency. Yet in recent years, the smallest U.S. coin has come under growing scrutiny, with economists, lawmakers, and consumers alike questioning its continued existence.

So why is the penny on the chopping block? And what would happen if the U.S. eliminated it altogether? Here’s a closer look at the debate.

The Case Against the Penny

1. It Costs More Than It’s Worth

The most frequently cited argument is simple economics: it costs more than a penny to make a penny. As of recent U.S. Mint reports, each 1¢ coin costs nearly 2.7 cents to produce, due to the cost of raw materials, manufacturing, and distribution.

That means the U.S. government loses millions annually just by making the coin.

2. It’s Barely Useful

The penny has lost most of its purchasing power. There’s almost nothing you can buy with a single cent, and even when prices include them, consumers often leave them behind or store them in jars.

In everyday transactions, pennies often slow down lines at stores and create minor inefficiencies without adding much value.

3. Environmental Impact

Because they’re made mostly of zinc with a copper coating, producing billions of pennies a year requires significant mining, energy use, and emissions. Eliminating the penny would slightly reduce the environmental footprint of coin production.

4. Other Countries Have Moved On

Several developed nations—including Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and the U.K.—have already discontinued their lowest-denomination coins. They rely on rounding systems for cash transactions (e.g., rounding to the nearest 5 cents), while card and digital payments remain exact.

The result? Minimal disruption, and often positive public reception.

Arguments for Keeping the Penny

Not everyone agrees the penny should go. Some of the arguments in favor of keeping it include:

  • Rounding concerns: Critics fear prices might be rounded up more often than down, disproportionately affecting low-income consumers.
  • Charitable donations: Many small-donation drives rely on pennies—like "penny wars" in schools or coin drives for nonprofits.
  • Historical significance: The penny features Abraham Lincoln, one of America’s most revered presidents, and has become a symbol of tradition.
  • Public sentiment: Polls often show that a majority of Americans like the penny and feel nostalgic about it, even if they don’t use it frequently.

What Would Happen If We Got Rid of It?

If the U.S. were to discontinue the penny, it would likely follow the Canadian model:

  • Cash transactions would be rounded to the nearest 5¢.
  • Electronic payments (credit/debit) would remain unchanged.
  • Old pennies could still be used indefinitely but would slowly disappear from circulation.
  • Retailers would have time to update pricing and point-of-sale systems accordingly.

In short, the penny would fade away gradually, not vanish overnight.

Legislative Status

Efforts to eliminate the penny have been proposed in Congress multiple times, but none have passed. The idea has bipartisan support in principle, but resistance from special interest groups (like zinc producers) and public attachment have kept the coin alive—for now.

The Bottom Line

The penny is a classic example of economic nostalgia vs. practical reality. While it holds historic and symbolic value, its actual utility in the 21st century is questionable. As inflation continues to erode its worth and digital transactions dominate, the penny may eventually go the way of the half-cent—into the pages of history.

Whether that happens soon or not, one thing is certain: the debate over the penny is far from over.
